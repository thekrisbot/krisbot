Bark Side of love

Chester and Elodie are K-I-S-S-I-N-G
April 29, 2026

Chester stood stiller than usual today. A soft, pink petal drifted down onto one of his branches. That stung more than it should have.

Across, Elodie shimmered in the morning light, ringed by admirers and camera flashes. The college kids were already back, taking selfies under her boughs as if she were some seasonal miracle. Which, to be fair, she was. Cherry blossoms always were.

Chester hated how graceful she looked. Hated how even the pigeons avoided branch-bombing her. Most of all, he hated how he couldn't look away from her even for a minute.

It hadn't always been like this. Once upon a time, kids swung from his limbs, families picnicked beneath his arms, and squirrels called him home. But after the cat incident  (one unlucky house cat chased too high, one complaint too loud, one orange tag reading Council Review Pending later) out came the verdict: "Hazard".

No more squirrels. No children laughing around. Just eerie silence.

And now, with Elodie blooming like some goddess of spring, Chester's loneliness felt watered daily.

"Dude, you still hung up on her?" came a rasp. It was JD, the trash can.

Installed in 2019, sporting a half-torn "London Borough of Lewisham" sticker, JD was "the most sustainable bastard in four postcodes," as he never tired of reminding everyone. He rolled closer, squeaking like a pirate who'd swallowed a radio. His gaze drifted up to the old orange tag nailed into Chester's bark. Council Review Pending.

"She's just pink fluff," JD continued. "Give it a month. Poof! She withers, like leftovers in me green bin."

"Ugh, here comes Katy,"  he groaned, pivoting dramatically.

Katy, the plastic bag, floated in like a confused butterfly.

"Do you ever feel," she sang, "like a plastic bag… drifting through the wind… wanting to start again?"

She twirled, caught a breeze, and landed neatly on one of Chester's lower branches.

"Stuck the landing this time," she beamed.

Chester sighed. Annoying as she was, he'd grown oddly fond of her; the same way one grows used to a squeaky gate or a flickering streetlight.

"She's not just fluff," he said, still watching her. "She leans toward toddlers when they drop their cones. I've seen it."

"That's called the wind, ya lovesick plank." JD dismissed.

Across the path, a teen reached up and plucked a blossom from one of Elodie's lower branches. He tucked it behind his girlfriend's ear. She laughed. A small crowd cooed. Someone took a picture.

Chester watched.

"You don't understand"  he muttered, bark creaking. "I'd rather fall to pieces than keep standing here invisible."

JD snorted. "Romance? Please. I've had that bloody pigeon try to nest inside me all day, like I'm some frickin' surrogate. Forgive me if I don't share your enthusiasm for courtship."

Chester muttered, low and splintered, "Kill me. Kill me now."

Across the path, two men in fluorescent vests were surveying the park. Clipboards. Hard hats. Chainsaws. They'd been circling for weeks.

"Oh no," Katy whispered. "You didn't just jinx yourself, did you?"

Engines roared to life. The workers approached. One checked the old orange tag nailed into Chester's bark. The other revved the blade.

Chester looked across at Elodie one last time.

A final pink petal drifted down, landing silently by his roots.

As the men began their work, JD muttered to no one in particular, "Well, I guess it's just Katy and me now."

Katy hummed softly, trying to cheer him up.

JD looked down at her. "Guess I better learn the damn lyrics to Firework."
