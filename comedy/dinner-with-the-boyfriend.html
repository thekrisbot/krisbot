Dinner with the boyfriend

A German chancellor is having dinner with his daughter's new boyfriend. What could go wrong?
May 26, 2026

CHARACTERS:

DAD. Late 60s. Living quietly under a new name. Trying, for once, to be a good father.

PAULA. Late 60s. His sister. Sharp, weary, watching from the corner table with a glass of wine. The voice of reason. Mostly.

JOHANNA. Late 20s. His daughter. Giving a chance to reconnect after years of distancing and rebellion.

IVAN. Mid 20s. Her boyfriend. Russian. Warm, enthusiastic, completely oblivious to the undercurrents. 

WAITER. Aggressively cheerful. 

SETTING: A modest restaurant. Eastern Europe, 1953. White tablecloths. A wine list nobody reads.

(Lights up. Paula walks in first with her arm linked through her brother's. Dad is sweating through the outfit. They stop just inside the door. Paula straightens his tie.)

PAULA: Stop fidgeting.

DAD: I feel like a man at a tribunal.

PAULA: Well don't. Alright, let's go over one more time.

DAD: (under his breath, scoffing) I am Mr. Schicklgruber. A retired accountant. I like walking.

PAULA: Perfect!

DAD: (muttering) Schicklgruber. Of all the names in the world, father had to keep that one.

PAULA: It is a perfectly respectable name.

DAD: Nobody fears it, Paula. Otherwise, I wouldn't have changed it to.. you know what.

PAULA: Shup! None of that nonsense. That is all in the past. Besides, you are not trying to intimidate anyone here. You are doing this for Johanna.

(Dad fidgets with his cuff. Paula puts a hand on his arm.)

PAULA: Listen to me. You said you wanted a better relationship with her. This is what it takes. You sit. You smile. You ask about his work. You do not bring up architecture. You do not bring up Wagner. And you absolutely do not bring up Vienna.

DAD: What if it comes up?

PAULA: Trust me, your daughter's Russian boyfriend in his twenties is not going to bring up the War.

DAD: (small) Fine, you are right. It's just... I really want this to..

PAULA: I know. That is why you are going to be wonderful tonight. Now sit. I will be right over there. Keeping an eye.

(Paula peels off and crosses to a small corner table where a glass of wine is already waiting for her. She sits with a perfect view of the main table. Dad walks to his seat. Sits. Folds his hands on the table. Unfolds them. Refolds them the other way. He glances at Paula. She gives him a tiny nod.)

(Johanna and Ivan arrive. They share a hug and settle down.)

JOHANNA: Dad. This is Ivan.

IVAN: (immediately too enthusiastic) Mr. Schicklgruber. Big moment for me, sir. I have heard so much.

DAD: (half rising, half not, sitting back down too quickly) Please. Call me Mr. Schicklgruber.

IVAN: (confused)That is what I said.

DAD: Yes. Right. Good. Good.

(Across the room Paula sips her wine. Steady.)

(They sit. Dad takes Ivan in. Ivan beams back. Dad's hand finds his water glass, knocks it slightly, rights it, withdraws.)

IVAN: Before anything else. Your daughter. She is extraordinary. You have done something right.

DAD: Thank you. So tell me about your work.

IVAN: (lighting up) Yes. So I am a photographer. I studied at the Academy of Royal Arts in Moscow. Fine art first, then photography.

(A small beat. Dad's smile does not move. Only his eyes do something. Across the room Paula sets her wine down.)

IVAN: (oblivious, charging on) If you will excuse me one moment, I am going to wash my hands to get the tram smell off me, if you wouldn't mind.

(Ivan stands and goes. Dad watches him until he is out of earshot. Then he turns to Johanna.)

DAD: (contained, hurt) You had to find the one boy who went to art school. The one boy in Moscow. You were always like this, Johanna. Always rebellious.

JOHANNA: (quietly, not looking up) Not everything is about you, dad.

DAD: I am just saying.

(A beat. Ivan returns,  as the duo get quiet.)

IVAN: Right. Where were we?

DAD: (forcing it, stalling) Tell me more about your photography, Ivan. What do you think...

IVAN: About...?

DAD: (struggling) Makes a good photographer?

IVAN: (enthusiastically, laughing) Oh, that is a great question. I have to say.. Concentration. It is all about concentration. For instance, last month I camped out in a swamp for five hours to get a shot of the Baikal Teal. A magnificent creature! You lose concentration for one second and the whole shot is gone. But I did not, of course.

(Dad goes very still as Ivan lets out a hearty laugh.)

DAD: (carefully, dabbing his lip with a napkin) Right. Yes. Beautiful.

JOHANNA: Ivan does beautiful landscape photography.  Tell him about the mountain series, sweetie.

IVAN: Oh yes. So I spent three weeks in the mountains last spring. I would go out at dawn, find a good position, and I would just shoot. Anything that moved. Left, right, centre. If it was in front of me, I was shooting it. I had five rolls finished by my first week! (letting out a boisterous laugh) 

(A beat. Johanna holding his hand. Dad awkwardly smiling.)

IVAN: Hahaha! Where are my manners? Here I am, spending all the time talking. Sir, please, tell me about your experiences.

DAD: (nodding slowly) That's not entirely—

(The WAITER interrupts.)

DAD: (relieved, quietly to himself) Oh thank god.

(The Waiter is aggressively cheerful. He hums an old tune under his breath. He sets down menus. He looks at Dad. Something crosses his face. He cannot place it.)

WAITER: (to Dad, clicking finger) Oh beautiful. Dad, daughter... boyfriend. Let me guess! You are meeting him for the first time, haha!

IVAN: Wow. That was spectacular.

(The Waiter stares at Dad for a beat too long.)

DAD: (warily) Can I help you?

WAITER: It's just you look so familiar, kind sir. Say, have you been here before?

JOHANNA: I don't think so. It's our first time here.

WAITER: Right, right, of course.

(He leaves. Dad watches him go. His shoulders drop when the waiter is out of sight.)

JOHANNA: He seems like a fun one. This should be interesting.

DAD: (eyeing the door the waiter left through) Mm.

(The Waiter returns with a dash.)

WAITER: Sir. I am so sorry. I cannot let it go. Perhaps I got my hair cut from you?

DAD: (a flinch, then forcing calm) No way.

JOHANNA: (barely containing the smirk) Hmm, did you own a barber shop that we don't know about, dad?

WAITER: (miming scissors near his own temple) Like this kind of cutting.

DAD: (knuckles whitening on the napkin) With all due respect, you are clearly mistaking me for someone else. Can we move on?

(Across the room, Paula gestures discreetly.)

WAITER: (not convinced) You are right. My bad, my bad. Are we ready to order?

(They order. The Waiter brings their plates back almost immediately. He places the dishes carefully and leaves. Ivan looks down at his plate. Then up at Dad.)

IVAN: Before we eat, sir. Do you want to  say grace?

(Dad freezes. The whole table waits. Beat. Too long a beat. Johanna closes her eyes for half a second. Across the room, Paula physically tenses.)

DAD: (a hair too loud) Race!? What? I have no idea what you mean.

IVAN: (rolling his eyes at the overreaction) The grace, sir. Before the meal? Johanna tells me you are a religious man.

DAD: (recovering, badly, mopping his brow with the napkin) Oh. Grace. Yes. Grace. Of course grace. No, no, you go ahead.

IVAN: (delighted, lowering his head) Perfect.

(Ivan bows his head and says a short, sincere grace under his breath. Dad does not bow his head. He is staring at Johanna. Johanna is staring at her napkin. Across the room Paula crosses herself out of pure muscle memory.)

IVAN: (looking up, beaming) Amen.

DAD: Amen.

(They begin to eat. Ivan, mid bite, holds up his food .)

IVAN: Mmm, delicious.  Why do you need to look beyond plants, I say.

(Dad is confused as Johanna clarifies.)

JOHANNA: Ivan is a vegetarian, dad. Just like you!

IVAN: How wonderful! You know whenever I tell people I am a vegetarian, they always make a face. "We've never met a Russian who doesn't like meat" and things. But honestly. Can you imagine? Putting another creature through that much pain. For what? Your taste buds? It is just barbaric.

DAD: (drifting away, looking far away) Yes. Yes, exactly. The screaming. The fear. The. The smell of the blood.

(Johanna gives him a look.)

IVAN: (to Johanna) I like him. You said he was going to be difficult.

DAD: Oh did she now?

(He picks up his glass, misjudges, knocks it. Water everywhere.)

DAD: Sorry. Sorry. So sorry.

IVAN: It is nothing, sir, don't you worry.

(The Waiter materializes again, as Paula has her face in her hand across the room.)

DAD: (quietly, to himself) Oh, not him again.

WAITER: Oh, I see we have Mr. Butterfingers here. Haha, I will take care of that in a moment, don't you worry.

(As he cleans up, he freezes and stares again.)

WAITER: I am sorry but it is really bothering me. I'd bet me nana's necklace that I know you from somewhere. Did you ever do a speech? Very passionate. Lots of.

(He does an arm gesture that is almost but not quite a salute.)

WAITER: Like that.

DAD: (very small) No. I am positive.

WAITER: (long look, clicking his fingers) Right. I will get there. Just give me more time.

DAD: (stressing) It's really not necessary.

(He disappears. Ivan watches him go, delighted.)

IVAN: That is one persistent man. So! We should toast. Sir, may I order us a bottle of wine? Something red?

JOHANNA: (quickly) Oh, dad doesn't drink.

IVAN: (mildly surprised) Oh, my apologies, sir. I had no idea. In that case, you must try the juice. My friend Rudi cannot stop raving about the orange juice they serve here. Should I get three?

DAD: (looks up very slowly) The juice?

IVAN: Yes. He said the juice here is exceptional.

DAD: (with enormous care, gripping the table) I love juice. Juice is.. Juice is wonderful. I have no issue with juice. Tell him, sweetheart.

JOHANNA: (under her breath, annoyed) Dad. 

DAD: (almost rambling, to himself) Who does not love juice? Crazy!

(A beat. Johanna rolls her eyes and sighs as Ivan looks puzzled. She is just watching.)

IVAN: (amused) Right. Should we get three?

DAD: Yes. Three juices. Wonderful.

(Long pause. Ivan eats. Johanna watches Dad. Dad stares straight ahead, dabbing his eyebrow as the Waiter comes back at speed. He is almost vibrating. He stops at the table. Points at Dad. Opens his mouth.)

WAITER: (excitedly) I have got it.

(The table goes quiet, as Dad's jaw drops on instinct.)

WAITER: I have absolutely got it. I knew it from the moment you walked in. The face. The energy. The stache'.

(Dad puts his fork down very slowly, sweating. A very long silence. Dad looks at Johanna. Johanna looks at her juice. Ivan looks between them both, thrilled. Across the room Paula slowly raises her wine to her lips and downs the entire glass.)

WAITER: (a wave of emotions crossing his face) Oh, what a fool I have been. Here you are, trying to have a quiet meal with your family, and I have been pestering you all night. I am so sorry. I am a fan, you see. A huge fan. I will say nothing more. If, only if, you would do me the honour of signing this.

(He produces a napkin and a pencil from his apron. Holds them out trembling. A very long beat. Everyone is confused. Dad hesitantly takes the pencil and scribbles a signature on the napkin and hands it back.)

DAD: (confused) Are we done here?

WAITER: (clutching the napkin to his chest) Yes, yes of course. Sir. Thank you. Thank you. Enjoy your evening. And please, please. The meal is on the house. It is the least I can do for the joy you bring to this world!

(The Waiter backs away from the table, still bowing slightly)

IVAN: (excited) What was that all about?

DAD: No godly idea.

(The party wrap up their meal and leave the restaurant as Paula follows them out from a distance as the Waiter  enters the kitchen door. A SECOND WAITER, younger, stops him.)

SECOND WAITER: (low, curious) Who was that?

WAITER: (hissing, ecstatic, like an idiot) You bloody fool. That is Mr. Charlie Chaplin. From The Great Dictator. I watched the movie three times with my family!

(The Second Waiter's eyes widen. The Waiter clutches the signed napkin and disappears through the kitchen door. Back at the table, Ivan is beaming.)

(Blackout.)
