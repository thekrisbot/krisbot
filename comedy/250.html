250

It's 1776. The Founding Fathers are fresh off making history, and they're toasting to what America looks like 250 years from now.
June 25, 2026

INT. INDEPENDENCE HALL, PHILADELPHIA - JULY 4, 1776 - NIGHT

The ink is barely dry on the Declaration of Independence. The room is warm, candlelit, bottles on the table. GEORGE WASHINGTON sits at the head, composed. JOHN ADAMS paces, still buzzing. THOMAS JEFFERSON leans back in his chair, satisfied. ALEXANDER HAMILTON has made his way for the celebrations.  

ADAMS

(raising his glass)

Gentlemen. We have done it. We have actually done it.

JEFFERSON

A hundred years from now, they will still be talking about what we pulled off!

HAMILTON

A hundred? Make that two fifty! And do the same with my drink. A double and a bit more! 

WASHINGTON

Alexander! What are you doing here? Shouldn't you be in New York?

HAMILTON

I took the four-thirty stage coach. I wouldn't miss this for the world. 

ADAMS

The more the merrier! 

HAMILTON

But seriously. Can you even imagine what America looks like in two hundred and fifty years?

ADAMS

(trailing off, animated gestures, counting on fingers)

Hold the two in mind..

(A beat. Everyone looks at him.)

WASHINGTON

(measured, ignoring Hamilton)

Twenty twenty-six, gentlemen. And the answer? Strong. Free. Governed by men of reason and principle. That is what we have laid the foundation for.

(A beat.)  

JEFFERSON

Naturally. The system we have designed makes sure of that.

HAMILTON

(pointing to his arm)

Look. Chills, gentlemen.

(A beat. They all nod.)

JEFFERSON

Speaking of which. We must comb over some of the more, if I may say so, controversial legislation.

ADAMS

Ah, here we go with the Second Amendment again. I told you. That stays. Every citizen must be able to defend himself. And a firearm will allow just that.

WASHINGTON

Agreed. I trust future Americans will exercise this right with the restraint and wisdom befitting a civilised republic.

JEFFERSON

You say that now. But tomorrow, say we name you our leader. And someone does not take to your welfare measures. Would you rather they take it out on you, bearing a firearm?

WASHINGTON

What a cartload of nonsense, Thomas. If I were leading this great nation, it is obvious that I am so widely beloved, so universally respected, that the very notion of someone wishing me harm is foolish.

HAMILTON

Precisely. What kind of man would our people elect who inspires that kind of hatred? Our leaders will always be dignified men. Educated men. Men of impeccable character. Nobody lifts a finger against such noble men, let alone a firearm.

JEFFERSON

(combing through the notes)

Fine. The Second Amendment stays. Now. The impeachment clause. Article Two. Should a president prove unfit, corrupt, or criminal in his conduct, Congress shall remove him from office.

WASHINGTON

A failsafe. Though I expect it will rarely if ever be needed.

ADAMS

Agreed. Our people would never elect a man of dishonour. Not now, not ever. Though, should we include a provision for such a character to not be able to represent the people again?

JEFFERSON

I would not concern myself with that, John. If by some extraordinary circumstance such a man were impeached, the people would most certainly never let him have a sniff of the office ever again.

(They all laugh. It is a funny hypothetical. They drink again. The room is warmer now.) 

HAMILTON

(leaning back, grinning)

Alright. Enough shop talk. We have changed the world tonight. I say we relax now. 

ADAMS

We are relaxing, my good man.

HAMILTON

No, no. I'd like to play a game. 

WASHINGTON

A game?

HAMILTON

Yes, something I picked up in our battle tents. Each man shall answer one question. 

JEFFERSON

Question? What question?

HAMILTON

If you were to lead our country, god bless. What is one thing you would do purely for yourself? No rules, no consequences! And I dare you to be truthful.

(A beat. The room shifts slightly. This is a different energy.)

JEFFERSON

(shifting in his seat)

Well. I suppose a man of principle would use the office to serve the people. To advance the cause of liberty and justice for all—

HAMILTON

Thomas, Thomas, sshhhhh, That is not what I am keen on. I want to know (pause, slurred) what you would do as the leader, if there was nobody watching. George, you go.

ADAMS

Ooooh, I like this game. (drifting off in his own thought)

WASHINGTON

Well if you put it that way... When I was a commander, I always regret that we couldn't slow down and cherish our moments together as a troop. 

(A Pause.)

So I guess some kind of a celebration with all of them would be a welcome trip down the memory lane.

HAMILTON

Like a pageantry? (excitedly)

(Washington stares into oblivion.)

WASHINGTON

(measured) Yes, yes, I would like that. A military parade down the main street. Cannons. Soldiers in formation. All of it. 

JEFFERSON

George! That is exactly the kind of monarchical indulgence we just fought a war against.

WASHINGTON

(slowly)

I suppose. But I thought that was the point of the game, Alexander? Forgive me for being a law-abiding man.

(They share a hearty laugh. Another drink.)

JEFFERSON

(shaking his head, then)

Alright, alright, Fair enough. Well in that case, if I were the supreme leader of the state, I would allocate a position of power for my good-for-nothing future son-in-law. You know? For my daughter's sake.

HAMILTON

Your son-in-law?

JEFFERSON

Thomas Mann Randolph Junior. Useless. Absolutely useless. Cannot organise a piss-off contest at a tavern. I would put him in charge of something far away and out of my sight.

ADAMS

(appalled)

Well goodness me, good thing that we chastise such dishonourable man who strives to enrich his own family like that!

(They chuckle.)

HAMILTON

Your turn, John.

(Adams straightens up. Looks around the room.)

ADAMS

(very seriously)

Everything.

WASHINGTON

(staring at him)

What do you mean, John?

ADAMS

I would take over everything!! Every building. Every bridge, every street, every monument!

(He holds up an apple from the table.)

ADAMS

This apple? Under my leadership, this would be called the "Adams Apple".

(lets out a raucous laugh. The rest all look at him in amusement.)

ADAMS

(not listening, already imagining it)

Adams Tower. Adams University. The Adams Grand Hotel. In gold lettering. Very large gold lettering.

JEFFERSON

What tyrannical vanity! (laughs) Alright then, Alexander. Let's hear your twisted, devious fantasy.

HAMILTON 

Funny you should ask. Don't mind if I do.

(Hamilton reaches into his coat and produces a crumpled piece of paper. He slides it across the table. Jefferson picks it up.)

ADAMS 

You have come prepared!

(Hamilton shrugs. Jefferson clears his throat and reads aloud.)

JEFFERSON 

Wow, the young boy has quite the list here:

Claim everything to the North.
Keep out everyone from the South.
Lock up anyone who dares stand against me.
Pardon anyone who stands with me.
And a third round if I feel like it. 

(Jefferson folds the paper carefully and places it back on the table as Hamilton is drunk and dancing.)

JEFFERSON 

First of all. You cannot simply claim everything to the North because you like it.

HAMILTON

When you are the most powerful man in the land, Thomas, you can grab whatever you like! Be it a northern duchy or a lady's pus—

(Jefferson spits out his drink. )

JEFFERSON

Okay, that is enough drinking for the night. (Takes the glass off Hamilton)

(A very long beat. The four of them sit in silence.)

WASHINGTON

(quietly)

Gentlemen. Do you know what I just realised? As we confessed our worst and most dangerous instincts, we have written an iron-clad constitution that will stand its ground against every tyrant, every egomaniac, every small man with large ambitions who mistakes power for permission. We have truly created something extraordinary tonight.

ALL

Hear, hear.

(They raise their glasses. A hopeful toast.)

WASHINGTON

To America.

ALL

To America!

(They drink. And quickly move out of the scene.) 

Present Day. 2026. July 4. White House. 

The  door bursts open as an ELDERLY MAN walks in excitedly.

ELDERLY MAN 

(classic hand gesture)

What a beautiful day. Two fifty. They said it was impossible. They said no one could do it. But I did it. You're welcome, Mamdani. (randomly pointing to the crowd)  Where is Mamdani? Oh that's right, I moved his seat to the parking lot. 

Anyway, To America.

(Blackout.)
