250

It's 1776. The Founding Fathers are fresh off making history, and they're toasting to what America looks like 250 years from now.
June 25, 2026

INT. INDEPENDENCE HALL, PHILADELPHIA - JULY 4, 1776 - NIGHT

The ink is barely dry on the Declaration of Independence. The room is warm, candlelit, bottles on the table. GEORGE WASHINGTON sits at the head, composed but flushed. JOHN ADAMS paces, still buzzing. THOMAS JEFFERSON leans back in his chair, satisfied. ALEXANDER HAMILTON, youngest in the room, is already on his third glass.  

ADAMS

(raising his glass)

Gentlemen. We have done it. We have actually done it.

JEFFERSON

A hundred years from now, they will still be talking about what we pulled off!

HAMILTON

A hundred? Make that two fifty! And do the same with my drink. Top up!

WASHINGTON

Alexander! What are you doing here? Shouldn't you be in New York?

HAMILTON

I took the four-thirty carriage. I wouldn't miss this for the world.

ADAMS

The more the merrier! But seriously. Can you even imagine what America looks like in two hundred and fifty years?

HAMILTON

(trailing off, counting on fingers)

Two in the mind, five in the hands...

(A beat. Everyone looks at him.)

WASHINGTON

(measured, ignoring Hamilton)

Twenty twenty-six, gentlemen. And the answer is: strong. Free. Governed by men of reason and principle. That is what we have built.

(A beat. Adams and Jefferson exchange a look, impressed.) 

HAMILTON

Naturally. The system we have designed makes sure of that.

JEFFERSON

(pointing to his arm)

Look. Chills, gentlemen.

(A beat. They all nod.)

JEFFERSON

Speaking of which. We must comb over some of the more, if I may say so, controversial legislation.

ADAMS

Ah, here we go with the Second Amendment again. I told you. That stays. Every citizen must be able to defend himself. And a firearm will allow just that.

HAMILTON

Agreed. Though I trust future Americans will exercise this right with the restraint and wisdom befitting a civilised republic.

JEFFERSON

You say that now. But tomorrow, say we name you our leader. And someone does not take to your welfare measures. Would you rather they bear a firearm to take it out on you?

WASHINGTON

What a cartload of nonsense, Thomas. If I were leading this great nation, it is obvious that I am so widely beloved, so universally respected, that the very notion of someone wishing me harm is unthinkable.

ADAMS

Precisely. What kind of man would our people elect who inspires that kind of hatred? Our leaders will always be dignified men. Educated men. Men of impeccable character. Nobody lifts a finger against such noble men, let alone a firearm.

JEFFERSON

(combing through the notes)

Fine. The Second Amendment stays. Now. The impeachment clause. Article Two. Should a president prove unfit, corrupt, or criminal in his conduct, Congress shall remove him from office.

WASHINGTON

A failsafe. Though I expect it will rarely if ever be needed.

ADAMS

Agreed. Our people would never elect a man of dishonour. Not now, not ever.

WASHINGTON

Should we include a provision for such a character to not be able to represent the people again?

JEFFERSON

I would not concern myself with that, George. If by some extraordinary circumstance such a man were impeached, the people would most certainly never let him have a sniff of the office ever again.

(They all laugh. It is a funny hypothetical. They drink again. The room is warmer now.) 

HAMILTON

(leaning back, grinning)

Alright. Enough shop talk. We have changed the world tonight. I say we celebrate properly!

ADAMS

We are celebrating, my good man.

HAMILTON

No no. I want to play a game. Each man answers one question, honestly, no judgment. I dare you to be truthful.

JEFFERSON

What question?

HAMILTON

If you were to lead our country. God bless. What is one thing you would do purely for yourself? No rules, no consequences, no history watching.

(A beat. The room shifts slightly. This is a different energy.)

ADAMS

(shifting in his seat)

Well. I suppose a man of principle would use the office to serve the people. To advance the cause of liberty and justice for all—

HAMILTON

John, John, dear sweet John. That is not what I asked. I want to know (pause, slurred) what you would do if there was nobody watching. George, you go.

(Washington sets his glass down. Looks around the room and jumps to answer.)

WASHINGTON

A pageantry! A military parade down the main street. Cannons. Soldiers in formation. All of it. Just for me.

JEFFERSON

Beg your pardon?

ADAMS

George. That is exactly the kind of monarchical vanity we just fought a war against.

WASHINGTON

(completely unbothered)

I thought that was the point of the game, Alexander?

(They share a hearty laugh. Another drink.)

JEFFERSON

(shaking his head, then)

My turn. I would allocate a position of power for my good-for-nothing future son-in-law. For my daughter's sake.

HAMILTON

Your son-in-law?

JEFFERSON

Thomas Mann Randolph Junior. Useless. Absolutely useless. Cannot negotiate his way out of a tavern. I would put him in charge of something far away and out of my sight.

ADAMS

(appalled)

Nonsense! The spirit of the republic? We must see to it that the presidency cannot enrich or empower your own family but strives to work for the good of the nation and only that.

WASHINGTON

Your turn, John.

Adams straightens up. Looks around the room. Picks up an apple from the bowl on the table. Turns it over in his hand.

ADAMS

(very seriously)

Everything.

HAMILTON

(staring at him)

What do you mean, John?

ADAMS

I would name everything after myself. Every building. Every institution. Every bridge, every street, every monument.

He holds up the apple.

ADAMS

This apple? Under my leadership, this would be called the "Adams Apple".

(lets out a raucous laugh.)

HAMILTON

(staring at him)

John. That already means something else.

ADAMS

(not listening, already imagining it)

Adams Tower. Adams University. The Adams Grand Hotel. Adams Museum. In gold lettering. Very large gold lettering.

JEFFERSON

What tyrannical vanity! (laughs)

ADAMS

(putting the apple down, satisfied)

That is why it is an indulgence, young boy.

HAMILTON

(grinning, leaning forward)

I would annex Canada. Rename it. New Columbia. No, wait. Something with flair. Americana.

(A beat.)

ADAMS

What about the treaty clause? The basic laws of sovereignty between nations? You cannot simply take a neighbour.

HAMILTON

I can grab whatever I feel like, Adams! Whether it is a next-door duchy or a lady's pu— 

(Jefferson spits out his drink. )

WASHINGTON

Okay, that is enough drinking for the night gentlemen. (Takes the glass off Hamilton)

(A very long silence. The four of them sit with that for a moment. Outside, fireworks.)  

JEFFERSON

(quietly)

Gentlemen. Do you know what I just realised?

ADAMS

You loathe the idiot your daughter is going to marry?

(A beat. They all laugh.)

JEFFERSON

(pleased, raising his glass)

Yes. But also. As we confessed our worst and most dangerous instincts, we have written an iron-clad constitution that will stand its ground against every tyrant, every egomaniac, every small man with large ambitions who mistakes power for permission. We have truly built something extraordinary tonight, gentlemen.

(They raise their glasses. A hopeful toast.)

WASHINGTON

To America.

ALL

To America!

(They drink. The candles flicker. Lights out.)

Present Day. 2026. July 4. White House. 

The door bursts open as an ELDERLY MAN bursts through excitedly.

ELDERLY MAN 

(classic hand gesture)

What a beautiful day. Two fifty. They said it was impossible. They said it couldn't be  done. But I did it. You're welcome, America.

(Blackout.)
